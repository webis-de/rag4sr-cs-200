Caption: Key measurements, main results, and general comments of included studies
| Study | Outcome measures | Experience measures | Results | General comments |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Appel et al. (2020) [W3000647430] | STAI, MiDAS, Standardized observations | Semi-structured interviews on VR experience | 6/8 negative items decreased; 6/8 positive emotions increased; 79%:“seemed very real”; 61% expressed enjoyment; 82%：easy to get used to HMD; No negative side-effects | Better image quality and increased narrative video content were suggested to improve the experience. VR content and equipment could be better adjusted and optimized to fit targeted population's needs. Participant selection was biased toward acceptance. No control group. |
| Ashmore et al. (2019) [W2915988192] | Custom survey on patient experience, Custom survey on staff experience | Custom survey on patient experience | 4 of the 5 children took MRI without GA. Patients reported feeling more positive about their MRI and would recommend the resource to other children. Staff assessed it could help prepare children for an awake MRI, by reducing anxiety. Both patients and staff rated the VR resource enjoyable, helpful and easy to use. | Small sample size without control group. Short intervention program without follow-up assessment. Low quality of measurements. |
| Coelho et al. (2020) [W3111901502] | Semi-structured interviews, Neuropsychiatric Inventory, EUROHIS-QOL-8, Guided observation | Guided observation (engagement), Simulator Sickness Questionnaire | No significant differences between pre- and post-intervention for psychological and behavioral symptomatology (M = 9.2, SD = 5.3; M = 9.7, SD = 5.3) and for quality of life (M = 28.6, SD = 5.9; M = 29.2, SD = 6.9). | One single patient does not allow generalizations about using 360° video interventions in hospital settings. |
| Evans et al. (2020) [W3004855555] | PANAS | Custom survey on acceptability and satisfaction | Increase in positive affect (27 to 37); Decrease in negative affect (19 to 12); Predominantly positive feelings concerning the technology | One single patient does not allow generalizations about using 360° video interventions in hospital settings. |
| Holmberg et al. (2020) [W3036229915] | SUDS, Semi-structured interviews (anxiety) | Semi-structured interviews (presence, acceptability, and cybersickness) | Patients with SAD have significantly higher SUDS scores than controls, in two of the videos. Presence was enhanced by sound and reduced by limited interaction. 8 out of 9 patients would want VRET as a part of their therapy. More cybersickness in controls than in SAD participants. | Small sample size. No data provided. |
| Hussain et al. (2018) [W2806459339] | Custom positive and negative emotions scale, ATSPPHS, SBISH | NA | More positive (M = 4.89, SD = .81) than negative emotions (M = 2.34, SD = .66), after watching the 360° videos. High positive attitude toward help seeking behavior (M = 4.34, SD = .96). More positive emotions marginally predicted a positive attitude for help seeking behavior (p = .052). | No baseline measurement or control group. The online surveys' content is not revealed. |
| Lindner et al. (2019) [W2884316046] | PSAS | NA | Between-group effect size (therapist-led OST + online program versus waiting group): d = 1.50 Within-group effect size (before - after therapist-led OST + online program): d = 1.67. Within-group effect size (before-after self-led OST + online program): d = 1.35; Significant decrease in PSAS scores post-transition to 6 months in the therapist-led arm (SE = 2.14, p = .007), but not in the self-led arm. | Relatively small sample. No systematic examination of psychiatric comorbidity. |
| Lundström and Fernaeus (2019) [W2983739368] | Standard CBT measurement | NA | Significant long-term effects (6 and 12-month follow-up) More meaningful work for caregivers. Increased mental presence, more spontaneous movements, general excitement of the elderly. Positively evaluated 360° VR experience. | No data or statistical methods provided. |
| Malihi et al. (2020) [W3039337668] | STAI | ITC-SoPI Self-report questionnaire for spatial presence, engagement, ecological validity/naturalness, and negative effects | Significant increase in anxiety after the HMD-VR task (Wilcoxon rank-sum: Z=183.5, p<.001). No significant difference between conditions. Significantly greater scores on spatial presence and naturalness for 360° video condition, compared to control, (d=0.3, p=.003 and d=0.47, p=.002 respectively). 74% of participants preferred 360° video condition. No significant differences for negative effects between conditions | The exclusion of children with a predisposition to motion sickness and the stationary scenario may bias the negative effects evaluation. |
| Nason et al. (2020) [W2990022891] | Semi-structured interviews, Ratings of anxiety | Ratings of motion sickness and immersiveness | Anxiety ratings: VR (M=4.33, SD =2.44); 360° video (M=3.63, SD =1.80) Immersiveness: VR (M=4.83, SD =1.69); 360° video (M=6.38, SD =2.50) Motion sickness: VR (M=1.33, SD =2.44); 360° video (M=2.50, SD =2.18) | Experience was slightly different in the compared conditions. Small sample size. Not enough statistical power to compare data across conditions. |
| Paul et al. (2020) [W3092340923] | PHQ-9 Self-reported depressive symptoms | TAM, SSQ, Five-question telepresence scale | Participants' depressive symptoms decreased from a score of 10 (moderate depression) to a score of 5 (mild depression). Average presence: 9.53 out of 12; Physical tolerability: 1.8 out of 48; Emotional tolerability: 3 out of 21 | Possible placebo effect. Change in medication. Single participant. |
| Reeves et al. (2021) [W3180638945] | PSAS, LSAS, FNE-B | IPQ | Significantly different PSAS scores between intervention and control groups at post-intervention (p < .001, η² = .52) and follow up (p < .001, η² = .95) Significantly different LSAS scores between intervention and control groups at post-intervention (p = .03, η² = .14) and follow up (p = .004, η² = .27) Significantly different FNE scores between intervention and control groups at post-intervention (p < .001, η² = .32) and follow up (p < .001, η² = .43) Significant difference in IPQ scores between emotion and neutral content groups (p = .01, η² = .27) Presence (β = .90, t = 7.13, p < .001) and speech duration (β = .30, t = 2.40, p = .03) significantly predicted changes in PSAS scores. | Not representative sample (female, white, young adults). |
| Tabbaa et al. (2019) [W2912685156] | OAS, OERS | Semi-structured interviews to reflect their experience. Qualitative observations | A substantial reduction of aggressive behaviors and a high level of presence was observed after 360° video experience. General alertness significantly increased from before (Mdn = 4.50) to after (Mdn = 5.00) 360° video exposure (p < .05). Significant increase in pleasure from before (Mdn = 1.25) to during (Mdn = 2, p < .05), and from before to after (Mdn = 1.75) 360° video exposure (p < .05). | Limited possibilities to adapt technological devices to individual needs. Relatively small sample. Single hospital setting. |
| Veling et al. (2021) [W3122860603] | EMA for affective states, PSS, IDS-SR, BAI, GPTS | SSQ, Time spent in VR | VRelax was significantly more efficient in reducing negative affective states, compared to relaxation exercises (16.2% versus 21.2%; t = -2.02, p = .04); Mean total score on SSQ was lower after (M = 43.1; SD = 10.9), compared to before VRelax (M = 48.3; SD = 12.7). | No measure of physiological effects of VRelax. Several participants reported cyber-sickness, and 2 stopped using VRelax because of nausea and dizziness. |
