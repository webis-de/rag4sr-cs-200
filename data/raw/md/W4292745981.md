# AERA Online Paper Repository

## Abstract

The purpose of this paper is to identify the merits of e-book usage for developing reading skills among emergent readers. By reviewing the most recent empirical research studies, this paper synthesizes the theoretical frameworks, the methodologies used, the reported educational benefits, as well as the differences in impact among age groups and experience. This review also determines implications for future research in identifying a need to establish guidelines for using e-books for literacy learning.

## Purpose

Early literacy skills set the precedent of reading enjoyment, therefore, it is vital to examine the effects that digital tools have on enhancing early literacy skills. The interactivity and features that are available in eBooks include (1) narration, which is text read by a digital voice, (2) hot spots, which are sections of text that perform some action when touched, (3) auditory features, or embedded music and other sound effects, and (4) visual features, or support that may include animation [1]. It is the interactivity of these features that call for research on the specific impact of eBooks for emergent readers. This systematic review study highlights the implications and preferred methods which should accompany eBook usage to achieve the greatest benefit by focusing on the following research questions:

1.  What theoretical frameworks do scholars use to lay the foundations of their studies?
2.  What methodologies and analysis techniques were used to examine the resulting data?
3.  What are the educational benefits of using eBooks for emergent readers?
4.  How may age and experience impact on the effects of eBooks?

## Theoretical Framework

The variety of theories used in this research branch from the motivation theory which decodes the reasons that influence learners to participate in learning activities. This premise is directly connected with the factors of interactivity that eBooks provide to keep the young readers engaged and self-motivated to continue the learning process [2]. In conjunction with the motivation theory, constructivism serves as a valuable perspective in this research since the learners can engage with the eBook's interactive components and construct their own knowledge. Additionally, learners can further construct knowledge socially alongside their peers [3].

## Methods

Identification of applicable studies was done using the ERIC database, Google Scholar, and the university library catalog. The key words used consisted of phrases such as "e-books and literacy", "digital books and literacy and emergent readers", and varied combinations of such. It was important to distinguish e-books from other digital media or applications which could include games and other platforms. The studies focus solely on e-books and digital texts. To answer the research questions, the following specifications for selection were established:

1.  Research must focus only on the use of eBooks as tools for literacy, rather than other technologies or tools.
2.  Research must use only participants who are early or emergent readers (grade 5 and under) in the age range from toddlers to early adolescence.
3.  Research must provide evidence, either by quantitative or qualitative methods, of (1) the results of eBook usage on literacy skills.
4.  Research must be published in peer-reviewed academic journals from 2008-2018.

## Results

### Theoretical Frameworks

Leveraging the eBook-literacy connection incorporates a wide-variety of critical theories across psychology, education, and communication (see **TABLE 2**). Studies with a focus on the motivation theory examined if and how eBooks increase the desire to read through orientation, intrinsic and extrinsic factors, social factors, and self-efficacy [2]. Since young learners have a cognitive reliance on the desire to learn, this theory showed great importance in terms of engagement with features of the eBook. The cognitive theory of multimedia learning is most referenced by the selected studies in that the presentation of words, images, and other items should be done in an organized way to bring about successful knowledge transfer [4]. The constructivist perspective supports the position that young learners use self-directed engagement to create knowledge [5]. The interactive features of the e-book encourage children to make their own choices to engage in the learning process.

The interactions that are available in e-books also grants young readers opportunities to engage with text in various ways that were not previously available [6]. This opportunity is supported by the theory of affordances, which focuses on the perceived possibilities for an action. This perception drives learners to act further, thus engaging in the learning process even more [7]. Long and Szabo [8] found that the transactional theory of reader response is vital in this context since e-book provides various opportunities of transactions between the reader and the text. They also found that the engagement theory of reading describes the mechanisms that cause learners to be engaged or disengaged readers [9].

### Methodologies

Six of the chosen studies focus on quantitative data, such as pre and post test scores, while the remaining 11 studies utilized a mixed method approach and included interviews, surveys or questionnaires. The studies were based in both formal school-day and after-school settings. The studies that used mixed methods not only analyzed scoring, but also observable attitudes and engagement. For instance, Zipke [10] found that although there was no statistical difference in word recognition and comprehension scores when using a paper or eBook, the participants were visibly more engaged with and showed enjoyment when using the eBook. This is important to know since enjoyment in reading will lead to increased reading frequency in general [11]. **TABLE 3** indicates a breakdown of the methodologies employed.

### Educational Benefits

#### Engagement

As expected, most of the studies were consistent in finding high levels of engagement with the eBook. Regardless of the outcome in comparison of performance results between paper books and eBooks, the studies find that children were still more engaged with eBooks. Evans, Nowak, Burek, and Willoughby [12] were able to find that while preschoolers performed better in alphabet recognition tests when using paper books, they were more visibly interactive with the eBook, touching and pointing to letters, while those with paper books rarely did. Another study measured motivation, attitude, and comprehension among second through 5th graders, in which the paper text outperformed the eBook once again [8]. Yet, when the participants were asked about their personal feelings, they mentioned enjoying using the eBooks in groups and that they chose certain books to download based off peer recommendation. Broemmel, Moran, and Wooten [13] also found no difference in comprehension or vocabulary growth, yet, the preschoolers were visibly excited, anticipating the next page and communicating their thoughts with their peers about what would happen next in the story. When children were given an alphabet eBook and alphabet paper book, findings echoed that there were no differences in scoring, yet those with the eBook spent more time focused on the eBook itself [14].

Though the eBooks were engaging, learners benefitted the most when the teacher was there to provide guidance [8]. Barnyak and McNelly [15] found that the areas in which there were the greatest gains were with those learners who had teacher support and whose peers were also involved in the eBook reading activity.

#### Access to stories

Merga and Mat Roni [16] presented a survey and interviewed an examiner to examine the relationship between eBook access and reading frequency. In this study, no correlation was found between reading was done, it took place on book-sized devices like the iPad rather than a phone or computer. This is a key finding when addressing the best environment for eBook usage. Though access in general is not a determining factor of reading frequency, choice appears to be a notable influence on participation [6]. Neumann [17] further cites that access to a greater number of stories allows children to take ownership of their literacy and through browsing also encounter letter and word recognition exercises as they try to identify which books they want to read.

#### Accommodating struggling readers

Another interesting revelation lies in the influence of eBooks on struggling readers. One study showed [5] that when elementary school students were given eBooks in an after school tutoring program specifically for struggling readers, they began to read for fun. One participant diagnosed with dyslexia noted that he listened to the audio feature for difficult words which made it possible to continue reading rather than stop due to frustration. He also indicated that he appreciated not having to get assistance from an adult since the audio feature supported autonomy. This further suggests that the interactive features embedded in most eBooks are beneficial in facilitating literacy learning [18].

#### Improving performance

As seen in **FIGURE 1**, the eBook was most valuable in overall performance and specifically in alphabet and word recognition along with print awareness. A notable difference shows in the task focus skill. None of the studies showed even scoring in this category, and out of the studies that examined task focus, most them showed that the eBook was more engaging.

### Effects of Age and Experience

The literature reveals that the ages of emergent readers play a larger role in performance. The results by Richter and Courage [19] noted the following differences (1) there was no difference in persistence for 3 and 4 year-olds, yet 5 year-olds showed a significantly higher amount of persistence with the eBook. Though the 3 and 4 year-olds did not persist with the eBook, (2) they were still more focused on and attentive to the eBook than the paper book and (3) there was no difference in enthusiasm in using eBooks with 3 year-olds, yet, the 4 years-olds were significantly more enthusiastic during eBook reading. Likewise, Strouse & Ganea [20], also found a performance difference in print awareness when using the eBook, with younger toddlers being unsuccessful and older toddlers recognizing the labels. These findings indicate that even those in the same age range will perform differently with both paper books and eBooks. Further, Korat & Segal-Drori [21] study not only found differences in results specific to the amount of times a story was read on an eBook, but also recorded the emergent literacy level so that results could identify any difference between higher levels of emergent literacy (EL) and lower levels of EL. This study ascertained that the more the child used or read the eBook, the better the performance was, irrespective of prior literacy level, to the point where scoring then surpassed that of the paper book when read a comparable number of times. This indicates not that eBooks are only suitable for a specific age range or experience level, but that young readers need more time and practice to familiarize themselves with the eBook features and that this varies by age.

## Scholarly Significance

The results indicate consistent benefits of eBook usage to assist in developing literacy skills for emergent learners. The data justifies the need to identify the amount and method of social support needed to gain the most benefit in building from the constructivist perspective that guides the thought behind this method. Additionally, studies that determine how much time and support children of various ages need to become familiar with using eBooks based on the skill they need to learn could be used to develop a guideline for early literacy programs.

Each study points to a need for child engagement to be the catalyst of learning literacy skills. Regardless of performance, the research indicates the increase in the engagement factor with use of eBooks, therefore, an accompanying guideline would benefit educators and parents alike. As early readers continue to be engaged, they also take ownership of reading ability.
